
After dimensionality reduction, each centred data point

$$
x_n - x
$$

is projected onto the first $\nu$ principal directions:

$$
y_n = Q_\nu^T (x_n - x),
$$

where
- $Q_\nu \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times \nu}$ contains the first $\nu$ eigenvectors of $S$,
- $y_n \in \mathbb{R}^{\nu}$.

The entries of $y_n$ are the coordinates of $x_n - x$ along the principal directions.

---
# 1. Reconstruction Formula

To recover an approximation of the original data point, we map back to the original space:
$$
\hat{x}_n = x + Q_\nu y_n.
$$

Substituting $y_n$:
$$
\hat{x}_n
=
x + Q_\nu Q_\nu^T (x_n - x).
$$

Thus, reconstruction consists of:
1. Centering the data,
2. Projecting onto the principal subspace,
3. Mapping back into the original $d$-dimensional space.

---
# 2. Geometric Interpretation

The matrix
$$
Q_\nu Q_\nu^T
$$

is the orthogonal projection matrix onto the $\nu$-dimensional principal subspace.

Therefore:
$$
\hat{x}_n
$$

is the orthogonal projection of $x_n$ onto the subspace spanned by the first $\nu$ principal components.

If $\nu = d$, then:
$$
Q_\nu Q_\nu^T = I,
$$

and reconstruction is exact.

If $\nu < d$, then some information is discarded, and reconstruction is approximate.

---
# 3. Reconstruction Error

The reconstruction error for a single data point is:

$$
x_n - \hat{x}_n
=
(I - Q_\nu Q_\nu^T)(x_n - x).
$$

This error lies entirely in the subspace spanned by the discarded eigenvectors.

---
# 4. Quantifying Loss: Frobenius Norm

Let $D$ denote the centred data matrix:
$$
D =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_1 - x \\
\vdots \\
x_N - x
\end{bmatrix}
\in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}.
$$

Let $D_\nu$ denote the reconstructed centred data matrix:
$$
D_\nu = D Q_\nu Q_\nu^T.
$$

The total variation in a matrix can be measured using the squared Frobenius norm:
$$
\|D\|_F^2
=
\sum_{i,j} D_{ij}^2.
$$

In PCA, an important result is:
$$
\|D\|_F^2 = N \sum_{i=1}^d \lambda_i,
$$

and
$$
\|D_\nu\|_F^2 = N \sum_{i=1}^{\nu} \lambda_i.
$$

Thus:

- Total variance in the data equals the sum of all eigenvalues.
- Variance retained after dimensionality reduction equals the sum of the first $\nu$ eigenvalues.

---
# 5. Proportion of Variance Explained

The proportion of total variance explained by the first $\nu$ principal components is:

$$
\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{\nu} \lambda_i}
     {\sum_{i=1}^{d} \lambda_i}.
$$

The proportion explained by the $j$-th principal component alone is:
$$
\frac{\lambda_j}
     {\sum_{i=1}^{d} \lambda_i}.
$$

The proportion explained by components $j$ through $k$ is:
$$
\frac{\sum_{i=j}^{k} \lambda_i}
     {\sum_{i=1}^{d} \lambda_i}.
$$

---

# 6. Interpretation of Information Loss

If we discard components $\nu+1$ through $d$, the lost variance is:
$$
\sum_{i=\nu+1}^{d} \lambda_i.
$$

Thus, the magnitude of the first neglected eigenvalue $\lambda_{\nu+1}$ gives an indication of how much additional variance would be captured by increasing the dimension.


If
$$
\lambda_{\nu+1} = 0,
$$

then the projection onto the first $\nu$ components is exact, and no information is lost.

---
# 7. Why Do We Reconstruct the Data?

At first glance, reconstruction may seem unnecessary:

- We project the data onto the first $\nu$ principal components,
- We obtain lower-dimensional representations,
- So why map back to the original space?

The reason is that PCA is not merely a change of coordinates.

PCA solves the following optimization problem:

> Among all $\nu$-dimensional linear subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^d$,  find the one that minimizes total squared reconstruction error.

More formally, PCA chooses $Q_\nu$ such that
$$
\sum_{n=1}^N
\| x_n - \hat{x}_n \|^2
$$
is minimized.

Thus, reconstruction allows us to:
- Measure how much information is lost,
- Compare different choices of $\nu$,
- Interpret PCA as an optimal compression method.

---
### Geometric Meaning

When we reduce dimensionality:
- We replace each data point with its orthogonal projection onto a $\nu$-dimensional subspace.
- The reconstruction $\hat{x}_n$ is the closest point (in Euclidean distance) to $x_n$ that lies in that subspace.

The reconstruction error is precisely the squared distance from the data point to the principal subspace.

---
### What This Result Implies

1. **Optimal Linear Compression**
   PCA provides the best possible linear approximation of dimension $\nu$.

2. **Eigenvalues Measure Information**
   The eigenvalues directly quantify:
	- Variance in each direction,
	 - Reconstruction quality,
	 - Information loss.

3. **Discarding Small Eigenvalues Discards Little Information**
   Directions with small eigenvalues contribute little to total variance,
   and therefore little to reconstruction accuracy.

4. **Exact Reconstruction**
   If the discarded eigenvalues are zero, the data lies entirely in a lower-dimensional subspace, and reconstruction is exact.

---
### Conceptual Perspective
PCA can therefore be viewed in three equivalent ways:
1. Variance maximization,
2. Orthogonal change of basis,
3. Optimal low-dimensional approximation.

The reconstruction step makes the third interpretation explicit.

---
# Conceptual Summary

Dimensionality reduction via PCA:
1. Rotates the coordinate system into the eigenbasis of $S$.
2. Discards directions with small eigenvalues.
3. Reconstructs data by projecting onto the retained subspace.

The eigenvalues quantify:
- Variance along each principal direction,
- Reconstruction fidelity,
- Information loss.

PCA therefore provides the optimal $\nu$-dimensional linear approximation of the data in the sense of minimizing total squared reconstruction error.
