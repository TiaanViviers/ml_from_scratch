 Although PCA was derived via Eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix,
it is often implemented more elegantly using the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the centred data matrix.

---
# 1. Centred Data Matrix

Let
$$
d_n = x_n - x,
$$

and collect the centred data points into the matrix
$$
D = [d_1 \; \cdots \; d_N] \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times N}.
$$

Note that the mean of the columns of $D$ is zero.

---
# 2. Singular Value Decomposition

The SVD factorizes $D$ as

$$
D = U \Sigma V^T,
$$

where:
- $U \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is orthogonal,
- $V \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is orthogonal,
- $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times N}$ is diagonal (possibly rectangular),
- The singular values $\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \cdots \ge 0$ appear on the diagonal of $\Sigma$.

If $N \ge d$, we often use the reduced SVD:
$$
D = U \Sigma_d V_d^T,
$$

where:

- $\Sigma_d \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$,
- $V_d \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ contains the first $d$ columns of $V$.

---
# 3. Connection Between SVD and PCA
Form the matrix:
$$
D D^T.
$$

Using the SVD:
$$
D D^T = U \Sigma \Sigma^T U^T.
$$

Thus:
- $U$ is the eigenvector matrix of $D D^T$.
- The eigenvalues are $\sigma_i^2$.

Recall that the sample covariance matrix is:
$$
S = \frac{1}{N} D D^T.
$$

Therefore:
- The eigenvectors of $S$ are the columns of $U$.
- The eigenvalues of $S$ are:

$$
\lambda_i = \frac{\sigma_i^2}{N}.
$$

### Important Conclusion

From the SVD of $D$, we directly obtain:
- Principal components → columns of $U$.
- Variances along principal components → $\lambda_i = \sigma_i^2 / N$.
- Standard deviations along principal components → $\sigma_i / \sqrt{N}$.

Thus SVD automatically gives PCA.

---
# 4. Interpretation of $U^T D$

Multiply both sides of the reduced SVD by $U^T$:

$$
U^T D = \Sigma_d V_d^T.
$$

This means:
- Left-multiplying by $U^T$ rotates the centred data into principal component coordinates.
- The transformed data has principal directions aligned with the coordinate axes.
- The spread along each axis is given by $\sigma_i$.

Thus $U^T D$ corresponds exactly to the PCA-transformed data.

---
# 5. Whitening via SVD
If all singular values are non-zero, consider:

$$
\Sigma_d^{-1} U^T D = V_d^T.
$$

In this representation:
- The covariance becomes identity (up to scaling by $1/N$).
- The data is spherical.
- All singular values become equal to one.

Thus SVD directly provides the whitening transformation.

---
# 6. Rank and Intrinsic Dimension

If only $r \le d$ singular values are non-zero, then:
- The rank of $D$ is $r$.
- The data lies in an $r$-dimensional subspace.
- The true intrinsic dimensionality is $r$.

The compact SVD is:
$$
D = U_r \Sigma_r V_r^T,
$$

where:
- $U_r \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$,
- $\Sigma_r \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times r}$,
- $V_r \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times r}$.

---
# 7. Dimensionality Reduction via SVD
Keeping only the first $\nu \le r$ singular values:

$$
D_\nu = U_\nu \Sigma_\nu V_\nu^T.
$$
This provides the best rank-$\nu$ approximation of $D$ in Frobenius norm.

The proportion of variance explained is:

$$
\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{\nu} \lambda_i}
     {\sum_{i=1}^{r} \lambda_i}.
$$

Since

$$
\lambda_i = \frac{\sigma_i^2}{N},
$$

this depends only on the singular values.

---
# Conceptual Summary

The SVD of the centred data matrix provides:

1. Principal directions → columns of $U$.
2. Variance along each direction → $\sigma_i^2 / N$.
3. Whitening transformation → $\Sigma^{-1} U^T D$.
4. Intrinsic dimensionality → rank of $D$.
5. Optimal low-rank approximation → truncation of singular values.

Thus SVD is a numerically stable and elegant way to perform PCA.
