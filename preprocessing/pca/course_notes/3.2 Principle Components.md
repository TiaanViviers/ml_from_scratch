### Setup
Suppose we are given $N$ observations

$$
x_{n} \in \mathbb{R}^d, \quad n = 1, \dots, N.
$$

We want to find the **direction of maximum variation** in the data.

---

## 1. Projecting Onto a One-Dimensional Subspace

Let $u_1 \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be a unit vector:

$$
u_1^T u_1 = 1.
$$

We project each data point onto the line spanned by $u_1$.

The projection of $x_n$ onto $u_1$ is:

$$
(u_1^T x_n) u_1.
$$
> Note: $proj_{u_1} x_n = (\frac {u_1^T \cdot x_n} {\| u_1 \|^2}) u_1$ but $\| u_1 \|$ = 1 (unit vector)


However, since we are working in the coordinate system defined by $u_1$,  the scalar projected value is simply:

$$
z^{(n)} = u_1^T x^{(n)}.
$$
So:
$(u_1^T x_n) u_1$        =    The projected vector in original space.
$z^{(n)} = u_1^T x^{(n)}$ =    The coordinate of the projection, a.k.a. **score**.

So PCA reduces to:
> Find the direction $u_1$ such that the variance of $z^{(n)}$ is maximized.

In PCA, we care about the *variance of the projected coordinates*, not the projected vectors themselves.
Why?
Because variance measures spread **along the line**, not in the original space.

---
## 2. Mean of sample and Mean of projected data

The sample mean is:

$$
\bar{x} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N x^{(n)}.
$$

The mean of the projected data is:

$$
\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N u_1^T x^{(n)} = u_1^T \bar{x}.
$$

---
## 3. Variance of sample and Variance of projected Data

The variance of the projected values is:

$$
\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N \left(u_1^T x^{(n)} - u_1^T \bar{x} \right)^2.
$$

Factor out $u_1^T$:

$$
= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N \left(u_1^T (x^{(n)} - \bar{x}) \right)^2.
$$

Now use the identity:

$$
(a^T b)^2 = a^T b b^T a.
$$

So we rewrite:

$$
\left(u_1^T (x^{(n)} - \bar{x}) \right)^2
=
u_1^T (x^{(n)} - \bar{x})(x^{(n)} - \bar{x})^T u_1.
$$

Thus the variance becomes:

$$
\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N
u_1^T (x^{(n)} - \bar{x})(x^{(n)} - \bar{x})^T u_1.
$$

Since $u_1$ does not depend on $n$, we factor it out:

$$
=
u_1^T
\left(
\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N
(x^{(n)} - \bar{x})(x^{(n)} - \bar{x})^T
\right)
u_1.
$$

Define the **sample covariance matrix**:

$$
S = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N
(x^{(n)} - \bar{x})(x^{(n)} - \bar{x})^T.
$$

Therefore, the **projected variance** (*variance of the scores*)is:

$$
u_1^T S u_1.
$$

---

## 4. The Optimization Problem

We want to maximize:

$$
u_1^T S u_1
$$

subject to the constraint:

$$
u_1^T u_1 = 1.
$$

Why the constraint?
If we did not constrain $u_1$, we could scale it arbitrarily and make the variance arbitrarily large.  
We are only interested in **direction**, not magnitude.

---

## 5. Solving with Lagrange Multipliers

Lagrange multipliers is a clean way of solving:
> Maximize something **while forcing a constraint**.

In the case of PCA:
> We want to maximize:
> $u_1^T S u_1$ 
> But only among vectors satisfying:
> $u_1^T u_1 = 1$


Define the Lagrangian:
$$
\mathcal{L}(u_1, \lambda)
=
u_1^T S u_1
+
\lambda (1 - u_1^T u_1).
$$

Differentiate with respect to $u_1$.

We use these matrix calculus identities:

- $\nabla_u (u^T S u) = 2 S u$
- $\nabla_u (u^T u) = 2u$

So:

$$
\nabla_{u_1} \mathcal{L}
=
2 S u_1 - 2 \lambda u_1.
$$

Setting gradient equal to zero:

$$
2 S u_1 - 2 \lambda u_1 = 0
$$

Divide by 2:

$$
S u_1 = \lambda u_1.
$$
$$\lambda = u_1^T S u_1$$

---

## 6. The Eigenvalue Problem

We have obtained:

$$
S u_1 = \lambda u_1.
$$

This is the **eigenvalue equation**.

So the stationary points of the variance occur when $u_1$ is an eigenvector of $S$.
For constrained problems, a stationary point means:
>The gradient of the objective is parallel to the gradient of the constraint

---

## 7. Interpreting λ
$$
\lambda = u_1^T S u_1.
$$
This shows:
> The variance along direction $u_1$ equals its eigenvalue.

So to maximize variance:
- Choose $u_1$ = eigenvector of $S$ corresponding to largest eigenvalue.

This is the **first principal component**.

---
## 8. Remaining Principal Components

After finding the first principal component $u_1$, we seek additional directions that:

1. Have unit length: $u^T u = 1$
2. Are orthogonal to previous components:
   $u^T u_1 = 0$

For the second component, we solve:

$$
\max_{u} u^T S u
$$

subject to:

$$
u^T u = 1,
\quad
u^T u_1 = 0.
$$

This again leads to the eigenvalue equation:

$$
S u = \lambda u.
$$

Because $S$ is symmetric, its eigenvectors are orthogonal.

Thus:

- The first principal component is the eigenvector with largest eigenvalue.
- The second principal component is the eigenvector with second largest eigenvalue.
- The third principal component is the eigenvector with third largest eigenvalue.
- And so on.

Each eigenvalue represents the variance captured in that direction.

Hence:

- Principal directions = eigenvectors of $S$
- Variance in each direction = corresponding eigenvalue

---

## 9. Eigenvalues of S are Non-Negative
For any vector $v$,

$$
v^T S v
=
\frac{1}{N}
\sum_{n=1}^N
\left(v^T (x^{(n)} - \bar{x})\right)^2
\ge 0.
$$

Thus $S$ is **positive semi-definite**.

A fundamental theorem states:

> A symmetric positive semi-definite matrix has non-negative eigenvalues.

Why is this important?

Because variance must be non-negative.

If eigenvalues could be negative, PCA would not make sense.

---

## Conceptual Summary

PCA finds directions that:

- Maximize projected variance
- Are mutually orthogonal
- Are eigenvectors of the covariance matrix
- Have variances equal to eigenvalues

Conceptually all we are doing is determining the covariance matrix $S$, then finding its eigenvalues and corresponding eigenvectors. 

Because covariance is symmetric:
- Its eigenvectors form an orthonormal basis.
- In that basis, covariance matrix becomes diagonal.
    
So PCA is simply:
> Find the coordinate system in which the covariance matrix is diagonal.

In that coordinate system:
- All features are uncorrelated.
- Variance is sorted largest to smallest.
