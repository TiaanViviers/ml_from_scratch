After performing PCA and retaining the first $\nu$ principal components, the transformed data is
$$
y_n = Q_\nu^T (x_n - x),
$$

where:

- $Q_\nu \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times \nu}$ contains the first $\nu$ eigenvectors of the covariance matrix $S$,
- $x$ is the sample mean,
- $y_n \in \mathbb{R}^{\nu}$.

The covariance matrix of the transformed data is
$$
S_y = Q_\nu^T S Q_\nu = \Lambda_\nu,
$$

where
$$
\Lambda_\nu = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_\nu).
$$

Thus:
- The components are uncorrelated,
- The variance in direction $j$ equals $\lambda_j$.

However, the variances are generally not equal.

---
# 1. Motivation for Whitening
In some applications, it is desirable not only to have uncorrelated components, but also to have equal variance in each direction.

If the covariance matrix equals the identity,
$$
\text{Cov}(z_n) = I_\nu,
$$

then:
- The features are uncorrelated,
- Each feature has variance 1,
- The data is said to be **spherically distributed**.

Geometrically, this transforms the covariance ellipsoid into a sphere.

---
# 2. Whitening Transformation
To normalize the variances, we scale each principal component by the inverse square root of its eigenvalue.

Define
$$
\Lambda_\nu^{-1/2}
=
\text{diag}\left(
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda_1}},
\dots,
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda_\nu}}
\right).
$$

The whitening transformation is then
$$
z_n
=
\Lambda_\nu^{-1/2} Q_\nu^T (x_n - x).
$$

Equivalently,
$$
z_n
=
(Q_\nu \Lambda_\nu^{-1/2})^T (x_n - x).
$$

---
# 3. Covariance of Whitened Data

Using
$$
Q_\nu^T S Q_\nu = \Lambda_\nu,
$$

we obtain
$$
\text{Cov}(z_n)
=
\Lambda_\nu^{-1/2}
\Lambda_\nu
\Lambda_\nu^{-1/2}
=
I_\nu.
$$

Thus the transformed data has identity covariance.

---
# 4. Geometric Interpretation

Whitening consists of three steps:

1. Center the data.
2. Rotate the coordinate system so axes align with principal directions.
3. Rescale each axis so that the variance in every direction equals one.

Therefore:
- PCA performs a rotation.
- Whitening performs a rotation followed by scaling.

---
# 5. Relationship to PCA
Whitening is not the objective of PCA.

PCA:
- Finds directions of maximum variance,
- Diagonalizes the covariance matrix.

Whitening:
- Uses the PCA result,
- Removes all second-order structure,
- Produces data with identity covariance.

Thus whitening is a covariance-normalization step built on top of PCA.

---
# 6. Practical Note

If the covariance matrix $S$ is positive definite (all eigenvalues positive), whitening can also be achieved via a Cholesky decomposition:

$S = L L^T$,     where $L$ = lower triangular matrix 

leading to the transformation
$$
z_n = L^{-1} (x_n - x),
$$
which also yields identity covariance.

This approach may be computationally more efficient in certain settings.
