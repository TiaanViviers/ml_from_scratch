We have identified the principal directions as the eigenvectors of the sample covariance matrix $S$, and the variances in these directions as the corresponding eigenvalues.

---
# 1. Eigendecomposition of the Covariance Matrix

Let

$$
S Q = Q \Lambda.
$$

Here:

- $S \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is the sample covariance matrix = $\frac{1}{N} DD^T$,
- $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is the matrix whose columns are the eigenvectors of $S$,
- $\Lambda \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is a diagonal matrix with the eigenvalues of $S$ along the diagonal.

Since $S$ is symmetric, its eigenvectors can be chosen orthonormal. Therefore:

$$
Q^T Q = I = Q Q^T.
$$

Equivalently, we may write the diagonalization as:

$$
\Lambda = Q^T S Q.
$$

The eigenvalues are arranged in non-increasing order:

$$
\lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \dots \ge \lambda_d > 0.
$$

---
# 2. Transforming the Data

Let $\bar x$ denote the sample mean:
$$
\bar x = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N x_n
$$

We first center the data:
$$
x_n - \bar x.
$$

Now define the transformed data:
$$
y_n = Q^T (x_n - x), \quad n = 1, \dots, N.
$$

Since $Q$ is orthogonal, this transformation corresponds to:
- Shifting the origin to the mean,
- Rotating (and possibly reflecting) the coordinate axes,
- Aligning the coordinate axes with the principal directions.

---

# 3. Covariance of the Transformed Data

The sample covariance matrix of the transformed data is:

$$
S_y = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N y_n y_n^T
$$

Substituting $y_n = Q^T (x_n - x)$:

$$
S_y
=
\frac{1}{N}
\sum_{n=1}^N
Q^T (x_n - x)(x_n - x)^T Q.
$$

Factoring out $Q^T$ and $Q$:

$$
S_y = Q^T S Q.
$$

But from the eigendecomposition:

$$
Q^T S Q = \Lambda.
$$

Therefore:

$$
S_y = \Lambda.
$$

---

# 4. Interpretation

The transformed covariance matrix is diagonal:
$$
S_y = \Lambda.
$$

This implies:
- The components of $y$ are uncorrelated.
- The variance along the $j$-th principal direction is $\lambda_j$.

At this stage, the dimensionality is still $d$.

We have only:
- Shifted the data,
- Rotated the coordinate system.

No dimensionality reduction has occurred yet.

---
# 5. Reducing the Dimension
Let $Q_\nu \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times \nu}$ consist of the first $\nu$ eigenvectors:

$$
Q_\nu = [q_1, q_2, \dots, q_\nu],
$$
where $q_j$ is the eigenvector corresponding to $\lambda_j$.

Then:
$$
Q_\nu^T S Q_\nu = \Lambda_\nu,
$$

where $\Lambda_\nu$ is the $\nu \times \nu$ diagonal matrix:

$$
\Lambda_\nu =
\text{diag}(\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_\nu).
$$

We now redefine the transformed data:
$$
y_n = Q_\nu^T (x_n - x).
$$

Now:
$$
y_n \in \mathbb{R}^{\nu}.
$$

Thus, the dimensionality is reduced from $d$ to $\nu$.

---
# 6. Component-wise Interpretation

Since
$$
Q_\nu^T =
\begin{bmatrix}
q_1^T \\
\vdots \\
q_\nu^T
\end{bmatrix},
$$

we obtain
$$
y_n =
\begin{bmatrix}
q_1^T (x_n - x) \\
\vdots \\
q_\nu^T (x_n - x)
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Thus the $j$-th component of $y_n$ is:
$$
y_{jn} = q_j^T (x_n - \bar x).
$$

This is precisely the projection of the centered data point onto the $j$-th principal direction.

Therefore:
- The transformed coordinates are the projections onto the principal axes.
- We retain only the first $\nu$ principal directions.

---
# 7. Information Loss
The total variance of the data is:
$$
\sum_{j=1}^d \lambda_j.
$$

After reducing to $\nu$ dimensions, the retained variance is:
$$
\sum_{j=1}^\nu \lambda_j.
$$

The discarded variance is:
$$
\sum_{j=\nu+1}^d \lambda_j.
$$

A natural measure of relative information loss is:
$$
\frac{\sum_{j=\nu+1}^d \lambda_j}{\sum_{j=1}^d \lambda_j}.
$$

In particular, if
$$
\lambda_{\nu+1} = 0,
$$

then the projection is exact and no information is lost.

---

## Conceptual Summary

Dimensionality reduction via PCA consists of:

1. Centering the data.
2. Rotating into the eigenbasis of the covariance matrix.
3. Keeping only the directions with largest variance.

The covariance matrix becomes diagonal in the principal coordinate system, and dimensionality reduction is achieved by discarding directions with small eigenvalues.
