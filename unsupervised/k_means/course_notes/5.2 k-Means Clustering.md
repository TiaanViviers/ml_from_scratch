# Problem Setup

We observe $N$ data points
$$
X = \{x_1,x_2,...,x_N\}, \quad x_n \in \mathbb{R}^d
$$

The data belong to one of $k$ clusters, but the cluster labels are **unknown**.

The number of clusters $k$ is assumed to be known.

---
# Cluster Representation

Cluster membership is represented using **1-of-k coding**:
$$
t_n =
\begin{bmatrix}
t_{n1} \\
t_{n2} \\
\vdots \\
t_{nk}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

where
$$
t_{nj} =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } x_n \in C_j \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

Each observation belongs to exactly one cluster. (*Hard labelling*)

---
# Objective Function

k-means minimizes the **distortion function**
$$
J =
\sum_{n=1}^{N}
\sum_{j=1}^{k}
t_{nj} \|x_n-\mu_j\|^2
$$
where:
- $x_n \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is the $n$th observation
- $\mu_i$ is the mean of cluster $i$
- $t_{ni}$ is the cluster indicator, defined by

$$
t_{ni} =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } x_n \text{ is assigned to cluster } i \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

Since k-means uses **hard labelling**, each observation belongs to exactly one cluster, so for each $n$,
$$
t_{ni} \in \{0,1\}, \qquad \sum_{i=1}^{k} t_{ni} = 1
$$

The objective function therefore measures the total squared Euclidean distance between each point and the mean of the cluster to which it has been assigned. The aim of k-means is to choose both the labels $t_{ni}$ and the cluster means $\mu_i$ so that $J$ is as small as possible.

This objective represents the **within-cluster sum of squared distances**.

---
# Alternating Optimization

Minimization of $J$ proceeds by alternating between two steps.

### Assignment Step
Given cluster means $\mu_j$, determine cluster assignments.

Each observation is assigned to the nearest cluster:
$$
t_{nj} =
\begin{cases}
1 & j = \arg\min_j \|x_n-\mu_j\|^2 \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

### Mean Update Step
Given cluster assignments $t_{nj}$, update cluster means.

Setting
$$
\frac{\partial J}{\partial \mu_j}=0
$$

gives
$$
\mu_j =
\frac{\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj} x_n}
{\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}}
$$

Thus the cluster centroid is the **sample mean of the assigned observations**.

---
# k-Means Algorithm

1. Choose initial cluster means $\mu_1,...,\mu_k$
2. Assign observations to nearest cluster
3. Update cluster means
4. Repeat steps 2 and 3 until convergence

---
# Hard Assignments

k-means uses **hard clustering**:
$$
t_{nj} \in \{0,1\}
$$

Each observation belongs to exactly one cluster.

---
# Data Normalization

Distance-based clustering is sensitive to feature scaling. Algorithm depends heavily on scale.

Common preprocessing:
- standardization
- whitening

However, rescaling may remove meaningful distance information.

---
# Local Minima

The objective function $J$ has multiple local minima.

Therefore the final clustering depends heavily on the **initial cluster means**.

---
# Binary Split Initialization

An alternative initialization strategy:
1. Start with two random cluster means
2. Run k-means
3. Identify cluster with largest scatter
4. Split that cluster into two
5. Repeat until $k$ clusters are obtained

---
# Formal Derivation of the k-Means Update Equations

The k-means algorithm seeks to partition the $N$ observations into $k$ clusters by minimizing the **distortion objective function**
$$
J = \sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{i=1}^{k} t_{ni}\|x_n-\mu_i\|^2
$$

Joint minimization with respect to both $t_{ni}$ and $\mu_i$ is difficult, so k-means proceeds by **alternating minimization**:

1. Fix the cluster means $\mu_i$ and optimize the labels $t_{ni}$
2. Fix the labels $t_{ni}$ and optimize the cluster means $\mu_i$

## 1. Labelling Step

Suppose the cluster means $\mu_1,\ldots,\mu_k$ are fixed.

Then for a given observation $x_n$, the contribution to the objective function is
$$
\sum_{i=1}^{k} t_{ni}\|x_n-\mu_i\|^2
$$

Since exactly one of the $t_{ni}$ values must be 1 and all others must be 0, the objective is minimized by assigning $x_n$ to the cluster whose mean is closest to it. Therefore,
$$
t_{ni} =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } i = \arg\min_j \|x_n-\mu_j\|^2 \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

Thus, in the labelling step, each observation is assigned to its **nearest cluster mean**.

## 2. Parameter Estimation Step

Now suppose the labels $t_{ni}$ are fixed. We want to minimize
$$
J = \sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{i=1}^{k} t_{ni}\|x_n-\mu_i\|^2
$$

with respect to the cluster means $\mu_i$.

To derive the update for a particular cluster mean $\mu_j$, we differentiate $J$ with respect to $\mu_j$.

Because $\mu_j$ only appears in the terms where $i=j$, all other terms vanish when differentiating. Therefore,

$$
\frac{\partial J}{\partial \mu_j}
=
\sum_{n=1}^{N} \frac{\partial}{\partial \mu_j}\left(t_{nj}\|x_n-\mu_j\|^2\right)
$$

Since $t_{nj}$ is constant with respect to $\mu_j$, this becomes

$$
\frac{\partial J}{\partial \mu_j}
=
\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}\frac{\partial}{\partial \mu_j}\left(\|x_n-\mu_j\|^2\right)
$$

Using the result

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial \mu_j}\left(\|x_n-\mu_j\|^2\right)
=
-2(x_n-\mu_j)
$$

we get

$$
\frac{\partial J}{\partial \mu_j}
=
\sum_{n=1}^{N} -2t_{nj}(x_n-\mu_j)
$$

To minimize $J$, set the derivative equal to zero:

$$
\sum_{n=1}^{N} -2t_{nj}(x_n-\mu_j) = 0
$$

Divide by $-2$:

$$
\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}(x_n-\mu_j) = 0
$$

Expand the brackets:

$$
\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}x_n - \sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}\mu_j = 0
$$

Rearrange:

$$
\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}x_n = \sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}\mu_j
$$

Since $\mu_j$ does not depend on $n$, it can be factored out of the sum:

$$
\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}x_n = \mu_j \sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}
$$

Finally, solving for $\mu_j$ gives

$$
\mu_j =
\frac{\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}x_n}{\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}}
$$

## Interpretation of the Update Equation

The quantity
$$
\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}
$$

counts how many observations are assigned to cluster $j$, while
$$
\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}x_n
$$
adds together all observations assigned to cluster $j$.

Therefore,
$$
\mu_j =
\frac{\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}x_n}{\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}}
$$
is simply the **sample mean of all data points assigned to cluster $j$**.

This is why the algorithm is called **k-means**: once the labels are fixed, the optimal cluster representative is the mean of that cluster.

## Resulting Alternating Optimization Procedure

The derivation gives the two update rules underlying the k-means algorithm:

### Assignment rule
Assign each observation to the nearest cluster mean:

$$
t_{ni} =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } i = \arg\min_j \|x_n-\mu_j\|^2 \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

### Mean update rule
Recompute each cluster mean as the average of the assigned observations:

$$
\mu_j =
\frac{\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}x_n}{\sum_{n=1}^{N} t_{nj}}
$$

These two steps are repeated until convergence.

## Important Insight

The k-means algorithm does **not** directly solve the minimization problem in one step. Instead, it repeatedly decreases the objective function by alternating between:

- optimizing the labels $t_{ni}$ while keeping the means fixed
- optimizing the means $\mu_i$ while keeping the labels fixed

This is the essential idea behind the k-means algorithm, and it is also why k-means can be viewed as a simple example of the broader **Expectation Maximization** philosophy.

---
# Connection to EM

k-means can be interpreted as a special case of the **Expectation Maximization algorithm**:
- assignment step → analogous to **E-step**
- mean update step → analogous to **M-step**

The key difference is that k-means uses **hard cluster assignments**, whereas probabilistic mixture models use **soft assignments**.
