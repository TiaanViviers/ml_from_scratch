We want to compute the **MAP estimate** of the logistic regression parameters $w$.

Recall the **negative log-posterior**
$$
\ell(w) = -\ln[p(w)p(D|w)]
$$

Using the likelihood of logistic regression and a Gaussian prior
$$
p(w) \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\lambda I)
$$

the objective becomes
$$
\ell(w) =
-\sum_{n=1}^{N}
\Big[
y_n \ln \sigma(w^Tx_n)
+
(1-y_n)\ln(1-\sigma(w^Tx_n))
\Big]
+
\frac{1}{2\lambda} w^T w
+ const.
$$

Minimizing this function gives the **MAP estimate**
$$
w^* = \arg\min_w \ell(w)
$$

---
# Gradient of the Objective

To find the optimum we set the gradient to zero.

The gradient of the negative log-posterior is
$$
\nabla \ell(w)
=
-
\sum_{n=1}^{N}
\left(
y_n - \sigma(w^T x_n)
\right)x_n
+
\frac{1}{\lambda}w
$$

Interpretation:
- $y_n - \sigma(w^Tx_n)$ is the **prediction error**
- the term $\frac{1}{\lambda}w$ comes from the **regularization (prior)**

The optimal parameters satisfy
$$
\nabla \ell(w) = 0
$$

This system is **nonlinear**, so it cannot be solved analytically.

---
# Newton–Raphson Method

Newton's method iteratively updates the parameters using
$$
w_{k+1} = w_k - H^{-1}(w_k)\nabla\ell(w_k)
$$

where
- $\nabla\ell(w)$ is the gradient
- $H(w)$ is the **Hessian matrix** (matrix of second derivatives)

---
# Hessian Matrix

The Hessian of the objective is
$$
H(w)
=
\sum_{n=1}^{N}
\sigma(w^Tx_n)
\left(1-\sigma(w^Tx_n)\right)
x_n x_n^T
+
\frac{1}{\lambda}I
$$

Interpretation:

- $\sigma(w^Tx_n)(1-\sigma(w^Tx_n))$   comes from the derivative of the sigmoid
- $x_n x_n^T$    captures the curvature contributed by each data point
- $\frac{1}{\lambda}I$   comes from the Gaussian prior (regularization)

---
# Positive Definiteness of the Hessian

To show Newton's method converges to a **minimum**, we show that the Hessian is **positive definite**.

For any nonzero vector $z$,
$$
z^T H(w) z
=
\sum_{n=1}^{N}
\sigma_n(1-\sigma_n)
(x_n^T z)^2
+
\frac{1}{\lambda}\|z\|^2
$$

where
$$
\sigma_n = \sigma(w^T x_n)
$$

Since
$$
0 < \sigma_n < 1
$$

both terms are positive, so
$$
z^T H(w) z > 0
$$

Thus
$$
H(w) \text{ is positive definite.}
$$

---
# Consequences

Because the Hessian is **positive definite everywhere**:
1. The objective $\ell(w)$ is **convex**
2. There is **only one global minimum**
3. Newton's method **converges rapidly** from any starting point

This makes logistic regression a particularly well-behaved optimization problem.
