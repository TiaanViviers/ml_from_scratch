In the previous section we estimated the parameters $w$ using **maximum likelihood (MLE)** and then computed
$$
P(C_1|x,w^*) = \sigma(w^{*T}x)
$$

where $w^*$ is a point estimate.

In a **Bayesian approach**, we treat the parameters $w$ as **random variables** rather than fixed unknown constants.

Thus the posterior class probability must average over all possible parameter values.

---
# Posterior Class Probability

The predictive probability becomes
$$
P(C_1|x,D) = \int P(C_1,w|x,D)\,dw
$$

Using the probability product rule
$$
P(C_1,w|x,D)=P(C_1|w,x,D)p(w|x,D)
$$

so
$$
P(C_1|x,D)
=
\int P(C_1|w,x,D)p(w|x,D)\,dw
$$

Two independence assumptions simplify this expression:

1. Once the parameters $w$ are known, the class probability does not depend on the training data $D$
$$
P(C_1|w,x,D)=P(C_1|w,x)
$$

2. The parameters $w$ are independent of the test observation $x$ given the training data
$$
p(w|x,D)=p(w|D)
$$

Thus
$$
P(C_1|x,D)
=
\int P(C_1|w,x)\,p(w|D)\,dw
$$

This means the predictive probability is the **average of the model predictions over the posterior distribution of parameters**.

---
# Computational Difficulty

The integral
$$
\int P(C_1|w,x)p(w|D)dw
$$
generally has no closed form solution.

Possible approaches include
- Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)
- numerical integration

In practice a common approximation is used.

---
# MAP Approximation

If the posterior distribution $p(w|D)$ is sharply peaked at its maximum $w^*$, then

$$
P(C_1|x,D)
\approx
P(C_1|x,w^*)
$$

where
$$
w^*=\arg\max_w p(w|D)
$$

This is called the **Maximum A Posteriori (MAP)** estimate.

---
# MAP Optimization

Define

$$
E(w) = -\ln p(w|D)
$$

The MAP estimate minimizes
$$
w^* = \arg\min_w E(w)
$$

Using Bayes' theorem
$$
p(w|D)=\frac{p(D|w)p(w)}{p(D)}
$$

Taking logs gives
$$
-\ln p(w|D) = -\ln p(D|w) - \ln p(w) + \text{const}
$$

Thus
$$
w^* = \arg\min_w \left[-\ln p(D|w) - \ln p(w)\right]
$$

Compared to MLE, the only difference is the **prior term $p(w)$**.

---
# Choice of Prior

A common choice is an **isotropic Gaussian prior**
$$
p(w) = \mathcal{N}(0,\lambda I)
$$

This assumes
- mean $0$
- identical variance in every direction
- no correlation between parameters

For this prior
$$
\ln p(w) = -\frac{1}{2\lambda} w^T w
$$

---
# MAP Objective Function

Substituting into the MAP objective gives
$$
w^* =
\arg\min_w
\left[
-\ln p(D|w) + \frac{1}{2\lambda}w^Tw
\right]
$$

The first term is the **negative log-likelihood** from logistic regression
$$
-\sum_{n=1}^{N}
\left[
y_n \ln\sigma(w^Tx_n)
+
(1-y_n)\ln(1-\sigma(w^Tx_n))
\right]
$$

Thus the final optimization problem becomes
$$
w^* =
\arg\min_w
\left[
-\sum_{n=1}^{N}
\{y_n\ln\sigma(w^Tx_n)+(1-y_n)\ln(1-\sigma(w^Tx_n))\}
+
\frac{1}{2\lambda}w^Tw
\right]
$$

---
# Interpretation

The additional term
$$
\frac{1}{2\lambda}w^Tw
$$

is a **regularization penalty**.

It discourages large parameter values and therefore prevents overfitting.

---
# Bayesian vs Frequentist View

Frequentist interpretation:
- Regularization is added manually to prevent overfitting.

Bayesian interpretation:
- The regularization term arises naturally from the **log prior**.

---
# Hyperparameter

The strength of the regularization is controlled by the **hyperparameter**

$$
\lambda
$$

This parameter is typically selected using a **validation set**.

---
# Why Regularization is Needed in Logistic Regression

In logistic regression we estimate the parameters $w$ by minimizing the **negative log-likelihood
$$
E(w)=-
\sum_{n=1}^N
\Big[
y_n\ln\sigma(w^Tx_n)
+
(1-y_n)\ln(1-\sigma(w^Tx_n))
\Big]
$$

where
$$
\sigma(a)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-a}}
$$
is the logistic sigmoid.

### Problem with Maximum Likelihood

If the data is **linearly separable**, there exists a vector $w$ such that
$$
w^Tx_n > 0 \quad \text{for class } C_1
$$
$$
w^Tx_n < 0 \quad \text{for class } C_2
$$

The optimizer can then **increase the magnitude of $w$** to make
$$
\sigma(w^Tx_n) \rightarrow 1
\quad\text{or}\quad
0
$$
for the training data.

As a result:
- the loss keeps decreasing
- the weights grow without bound
$$
||w|| \rightarrow \infty
$$

Even though the **decision boundary**
$$
w^Tx=0
$$

remains unchanged.

### Consequences

Very large weights lead to
- extreme confidence on the training data
- numerical instability
- **overfitting**

The model becomes overly sensitive to small variations in the data.

### Regularization

To prevent this, a penalty term is added:
$$
\frac{1}{2\lambda}w^Tw
$$

The new objective becomes
$$
E(w)=
-\ln p(D|w)
+
\frac{1}{2\lambda}w^Tw
$$

This discourages large weights and improves generalization.

### Bayesian Interpretation

In the Bayesian framework we place a **Gaussian prior on the parameters**
$$
p(w)=\mathcal{N}(0,\lambda I)
$$

Taking logs gives
$$
\ln p(w)=-\frac{1}{2\lambda}w^Tw
$$

Thus the regularization term arises naturally from the **log-prior** in the MAP estimate.


Regularization balances two goals:
1. Fit the training data (likelihood term)
2. Keep the model simple (small weights)

This prevents the weights from growing arbitrarily large and reduces overfitting.

---
# Key Insight

The Bayesian approach leads to the same optimization problem as **regularized logistic regression**, but the regularization term now has a clear probabilistic interpretation: it corresponds to placing a **Gaussian prior on the parameters**.
