Assume the class-conditional densities are Gaussian:

$$
p(x|C_j) =
\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}
\exp\left(
-\frac12 (x-\mu_j)^T \Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_j)
\right)
$$

Each class has its own mean $\mu_j$ but **all classes share the same covariance matrix**

$$
\Sigma_1 = \Sigma_2 = \Sigma
$$

---
# Two-Class Case

Assume
$$
p(x|C_j)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}
\exp\left(
-\frac12 (x-\mu_j)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_j)
\right),
\qquad j=1,2
$$
with a **shared covariance matrices:** $\Sigma_1=\Sigma_2=\Sigma.$

We know from the previous section that for two classes
$$
P(C_1|x)=\sigma(a(x)),
$$

where
$$
a(x)=\ln\frac{p(x|C_1)P(C_1)}{p(x|C_2)P(C_2)}.
$$

Our goal is to determine the form of $a(x)$ under the Gaussian assumption.

### Step 1: Split the log into likelihood and prior terms

Using $\ln(AB)=\ln A + \ln B$ and $\ln(A/B)=\ln A-\ln B$,
$$
a(x)
=
\ln p(x|C_1)-\ln p(x|C_2)+\ln\frac{P(C_1)}{P(C_2)}.
$$

So the log-posterior odds are built from:
1. how well each class explains $x$
2. how likely each class was before seeing $x$

### Step 2: Substitute the Gaussian densities

For class $C_1$,
$$
\ln p(x|C_1)
=
\ln\left[
\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}
\exp\left(
-\frac12 (x-\mu_1)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_1)
\right)
\right].
$$
$$
\ln p(x|C_1)
=
\ln\left(\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}\right)
-\frac12 (x-\mu_1)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_1).
$$

Similarly,
$$
\ln p(x|C_2)
=
\ln\left(\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}\right)
-\frac12 (x-\mu_2)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_2).
$$

Substituting both into $a(x)$ gives
$$
a(x)
=
\ln\left(\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}\right)
-\frac12 (x-\mu_1)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_1)
$$
$$
\qquad
-\left[
\ln\left(\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}\right)
-\frac12 (x-\mu_2)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_2)
\right]
+\ln\frac{P(C_1)}{P(C_2)}.
$$

### Step 3: Cancel the normalizing constants

$$
a(x)
=
-\frac12 (x-\mu_1)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_1)
+\frac12 (x-\mu_2)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_2)
+\ln\frac{P(C_1)}{P(C_2)}.
$$

This cancellation is important:
- it happens because the covariance matrix is the same in both classes
- if the covariance matrices were different, these terms would not cancel completely

### Step 4: Expand the quadratic forms

Use the identity
$$
(x-\mu)^T A (x-\mu)
=
x^T A x - 2\mu^T A x + \mu^T A \mu
$$

So
$$
(x-\mu_1)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_1)
=
x^T\Sigma^{-1}x - 2\mu_1^T\Sigma^{-1}x + \mu_1^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_1
$$

and
$$
(x-\mu_2)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu_2)
=
x^T\Sigma^{-1}x - 2\mu_2^T\Sigma^{-1}x + \mu_2^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_2.
$$

Substitute these into $a(x)$:

$$
a(x)
=
-\frac12\left(
x^T\Sigma^{-1}x - 2\mu_1^T\Sigma^{-1}x + \mu_1^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_1
\right)
$$
$$
\qquad
+\frac12\left(
x^T\Sigma^{-1}x - 2\mu_2^T\Sigma^{-1}x + \mu_2^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_2
\right)
+\ln\frac{P(C_1)}{P(C_2)}.
$$
$$
a(x)
=
-\frac12 x^T\Sigma^{-1}x
+\mu_1^T\Sigma^{-1}x
-\frac12\mu_1^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_1
+\frac12 x^T\Sigma^{-1}x
-\mu_2^T\Sigma^{-1}x
+\frac12\mu_2^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_2
+\ln\frac{P(C_1)}{P(C_2)}.
$$

### Step 5: Cancel the quadratic terms in $x$

Now the crucial step:
$$
-\frac12 x^T\Sigma^{-1}x + \frac12 x^T\Sigma^{-1}x = 0.
$$

So the quadratic terms in $x$ cancel.

This is the most important observation in the derivation.

Because the covariance matrix is shared, the log-posterior odds are no longer quadratic in $x$; they reduce to a linear function of $x$.

After cancellation we get
$$
a(x)
=
\mu_1^T\Sigma^{-1}x
-\mu_2^T\Sigma^{-1}x
-\frac12\mu_1^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_1
+\frac12\mu_2^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_2
+\ln\frac{P(C_1)}{P(C_2)}.
$$
$$
a(x)
=
(\mu_1-\mu_2)^T\Sigma^{-1}x
-\frac12\mu_1^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_1
+\frac12\mu_2^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_2
+\ln\frac{P(C_1)}{P(C_2)}.
$$

Using the scalar identity
$$
(\mu_1-\mu_2)^T\Sigma^{-1}x
=
\left[\Sigma^{-1}(\mu_1-\mu_2)\right]^T x,
$$

we can write this in the standard linear form
$$
a(x)=w^T x+w_0,
$$

where
$$
w=\Sigma^{-1}(\mu_1-\mu_2)
$$
$$
w_0=
-\frac12\mu_1^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_1
+\frac12\mu_2^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_2
+\ln\frac{P(C_1)}{P(C_2)}.
$$

### Step 6: Final posterior probability

Substituting this into the sigmoid form gives
$$
P(C_1|x)=\sigma(w^T x+w_0),
$$

where
$$
\sigma(a)=\frac{1}{1+\exp(-a)}.
$$

So under the assumption of Gaussian class-conditionals with shared covariance, the posterior probability has the form of a sigmoid applied to a linear function of $x$.

---
# Important observations

### 1. Why the classifier is linear

The classifier is called **linear** because
$$
a(x)=w^T x+w_0
$$
is linear in $x$.

Equivalently, the decision boundary will be given by a first-order equation in $x$.
It refers to the shape of the decision boundary.

### 2. Where the prior enters

The prior probabilities only appear in the bias/intercept term
$$
w_0=
-\frac12\mu_1^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_1
+\frac12\mu_2^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_2
+\ln\frac{P(C_1)}{P(C_2)}.
$$

So changing the priors shifts the decision boundary, but does not change the direction vector $w$.

### 3. Why shared covariance matters

If the covariance matrices were not shared, then the quadratic terms in $x$ would not cancel.

In that case $a(x)$ would contain terms of the form
$$
x^T A x,
$$
and the decision boundary would be **quadratic**, not linear.

---
# Decision Boundary

The decision boundary occurs when
$$
P(C_1|x)=P(C_2|x) = 1-P(C_1|x)
$$

This gives
$$
\sigma(w^Tx+w_0) = 1 - \sigma(w^Tx+w_0)
$$
$$
2\sigma(w^Tx+w_0) = 1
$$
$$
\sigma(w^Tx+w_0) = \frac12
$$
$$
\sigma^{-1}(
\sigma(w^Tx+w_0))
= \sigma^{-1}(\frac12)
$$
> Using the Logit: $\quad \sigma^{-1}(p) = \ln (\frac{p}{1-p})$:
$$
w^Tx+w_0
= \ln (\frac{0.5}{1-0.5})
= \ln (1)
$$
$$
w^Tx+w_0=0
$$

Thus the decision boundary is a **hyperplane**.

# Parameter Reduction

##### Generative:
Assuming $d$ features, $k$ classes and equal class Covariances:
- mean of each class: $\quad kd$ 
- Shared covariance matrix: $\quad \frac{d(d+1)}{2}$
- Priors: $\quad k-1$

> Note: 
	Covariance is a $d$x$d$ symmetric matrix, we only estimate upper triangle
	Class priors must sum to 1, so we estimate k-1, last prior is implied by remainder.

The total number of parameters to estimate:
$$
kd + \frac{d(d+1)}{2} + (k-1)
$$

If covariances where not shared, $d$ features, $k$ classes (QDA):
- mean of each class: $\quad kd$ 
- Shared covariance matrix: $\quad k\frac{d(d+1)}{2}$
- Priors: $\quad k-1$

> Note:
> 	We need to estimate a covariance matrix for each class

The total number of parameters to estimate:
$$
kd + k\frac{d(d+1)}{2} + (k-1)
$$

##### Discriminative: (softmax logistic regression)
The total number of parameters to estimate is:
$$
(k-1)(d+1)
$$
This motivates directly estimating $w$ and $w_0$ in logistic regression.

# Multi-Class Case

For $k$ classes
$$
P(C_n|x)
=
\frac{\exp(a_n(x))}
{\sum_{j=1}^k \exp(a_j(x))}
$$

where
$$
a_j(x)=w_j^T x+w_{j0}
$$

with
$$
w_j=\Sigma^{-1}\mu_j
$$
$$
w_{j0}=
-\frac12\mu_j^T\Sigma^{-1}\mu_j
+
\ln P(C_j)
$$

This produces **linear decision boundaries**.

---
# Non-Shared Covariance

If   $\Sigma_1 \neq \Sigma_2$:
- quadratic terms in $x$ remain.
- produces **quadratic decision boundaries**.

This model is known as **Quadratic Discriminant Analysis (QDA)**.
